The Republic falls

Reform, uprising, and partition in the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, 1648–1795

Source-led surveyEight original excerpts with new English translationsPrint edition · 2026

Reader guide

How a great republic disappeared from the map

In 1648 the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth was the largest state in Europe by territory; by 1795 it had been erased from the map by its neighbours. The century between is neither a simple tragedy nor a heroic prelude to modern nationalism. It is the history of a political system that expanded participation while weakening enforcement, waged wars it could not afford, and produced reforms it could not defend.

This companion follows the Commonwealth from the Khmelnytsky uprising through the Swedish Deluge, the Saxon kings, the Enlightenment, the Constitution of 3 May 1791, and the final insurrections. The central question is the same as in earlier chapters: whose liberty was protected, whose labour was exploited, and whose voice was heard when the state faced its greatest crises? The sources come from the political actors themselves: Cossack petitions, royal oaths, treaties, constitutional articles, confederation acts, and insurrectionary proclamations. Eight are displayed in the original Polish with new English translations.

Every quoted excerpt is collated from a named primary edition. The 1648 General Confederation is read from the 1859 Volumina legum reprint; Khmelnytsky’s 1649 letter from the 1864 edition of Jakub Michałowski’s diary; the Lwów Oath, the Hadziach Treaty, the 1768 Russian treaty, the Targowica act, and the Polaniec Manifesto from Polish Wikisource transcriptions of public-domain texts; the Constitution of 3 May from the 1885 Echo z nad Sanu reprint. Translations preserve the voice of the original, including its legal formulas and political assumptions.

01 · Orientation

What was at stake?

The Commonwealth of 1648 was not a doomed empire. Its territory stretched from the Baltic to the Black Sea, its grain fed Amsterdam and Venice, its Sejm was Europe’s largest noble assembly, and its confessional order was more diverse than almost any of its neighbours. Yet three structural problems ran through the century: the state could not tax enough to fight, the king could not govern without the consent of a fractious nobility, and the neighbours—Russia, Brandenburg-Prussia, Austria—were becoming more centralised while the Commonwealth was not.

The collapse was not a single battle. It was a long process in which the Commonwealth’s own political culture—its cult of liberty, its veto, its suspicion of executive power—became instruments that foreign powers could manipulate. The story is therefore not only about invasion; it is about how a republic that valued freedom above coercion found itself unable to coerce in its own defence.

02 · Uprising

The Cossack storm, 1648–1654

The Khmelnytsky uprising was the largest internal explosion the Commonwealth had yet faced. It combined Cossack grievances over register and status, Orthodox resentment against a Catholic-dominated church order, peasant flight and resistance on the southeastern frontier, and noble-magnate land-grabbing. It was not simply a Ukrainian national revolt, nor a Russian plot, nor a peasant jacquerie; it was all of these pressures at once, and its leader was both a rebel against the king and a man who insisted he had been loyal.

The registered Cossacks were a privileged military corps on the Dnieper. The Commonwealth wanted to cut their numbers, subject them to Polish officers, and confine their trade and privileges. Khmelnytsky himself had been a registered Cossack officer who lost his estate to a Polish magnate. In spring 1648 he escaped Polish arrest, reached the Zaporozhian Sich, and was elected hetman. Within months his forces, allied with Crimean Tatars, destroyed Polish armies at Zhovti Vody and Korsun. The Crown hetmans were captured; the southeast was in flames.

The Sejm’s response in 1648 was both military and diplomatic. The General Confederation authorised a commission to negotiate with the Cossacks while mobilising the nobility. The language of the act is revealing: the Cossack disturbances are to be "thoroughly pacified," but the commission is also empowered to settle whatever it deems necessary, with confirmation deferred until the election of a new king. The Commonwealth was fighting, bargaining, and postponing decisions at the same time.

The act treats Cossack unrest as a matter of pacification and negotiation, not as a foreign war. That framing made sense to the Commonwealth’s political nation—Cossacks were subjects and soldiers—but it underestimated the scale of the revolt and the depth of its grievances.

Khmelnytsky’s letter is a masterwork of self-presentation. He denies rebellion, insists on loyalty, and explains his alliance with the Crimean khan as a petition for royal mercy. The document cannot be read naively as a statement of innocence, but it shows how the conflict was framed by both sides: Cossacks as loyal subjects driven to extremes, the Commonwealth as a state trying to discipline a frontier army.

Was 1648 a national revolution or a frontier rebellion?

Both readings exist in the sources. The Commonwealth’s acts speak of disturbances and pacification; Khmelnytsky speaks of grievance and exile. Modern historians stress the uprising’s long-term effect in opening Ukrainian lands to Muscovite influence, but contemporaries did not yet see it as the founding of a separate nation-state. The companion keeps both vocabularies in play.

03 · Invasion

The Deluge, 1655–1660

No sooner had the Cossack fire begun to spread than the Commonwealth was invaded from the north. In 1655 Charles X Gustav of Sweden swept through a country already exhausted and under-defended, and within months the king, the nobility, and the army were scattered. The Russian invasion, a Transylvanian raid, and local pogroms followed. Poland-Lithuania experienced a collapse of central authority that contemporaries called the Deluge.

The response was equally remarkable. The siege of the monastery at Częstochowa became a symbol of resistance; guerrilla warfare under Stefan Czarniecki and others tied down Swedish garrisons; and the papal nuncio helped raise funds across Catholic Europe. In 1656 John II Casimir, in exile at Lwów, made a solemn vow to the Virgin Mary, promising to place himself, the kingdom, and the common people under her protection and to defend the oppressed peasantry after peace returned.

Event: Lwów, 1 April 1656. Composition: royal vow before the icon of the Black Madonna at Lwów Cathedral. Witness: Polish Wikisource transcription, "Śluby lwowskie." Polish Wikisource.

The oath is simultaneously personal, political, and social. It identifies the Virgin Mary as the true patroness of the kingdom, binds the king’s legitimacy to her protection, and promises relief to peasants whose suffering John II Casimir recognises as a divine punishment. The promise was rarely kept; it nonetheless shows how a monarchy under siege used religious language to mobilise a society that the state itself could not protect.

Religion and survival

The Deluge made visible a pattern that would recur: when the regular state failed, religious symbols, local militias, and foreign subsidies filled the gap. The Commonwealth survived not because its central institutions worked, but because enough local societies kept fighting until the invaders overextended.

04 · Settlement

The eastern frontier: Hadziach and after

After the Deluge the Commonwealth tried to settle with the Cossacks. The Hadziach Treaty of 1658 offered the Zaporozhian Host a separate Rus’ palatinate within the Commonwealth, a Ruthenian prince, religious equality, and a greatly enlarged Cossack register. It was the most generous offer ever made to the Cossacks—and it failed.

Why it failed is debated. The Commonwealth was too weak to enforce it; the Cossack leadership was divided; Muscovy, having made gains in the war, was not prepared to see a Rus’ palatinate created as a Polish satellite; and the Polish nobility, having defeated the Cossacks on the battlefield, was reluctant to grant what the treaty promised. The result was a stalemate that ended with the 1667 Truce of Andrusovo, splitting the Cossack lands along the Dnieper: Left Bank under Muscovy, Right Bank under the Commonwealth, with Kyiv temporarily conceded to Muscovy for two years—two years that became permanent.

Event: Hadziach, 16 September 1658. Composition: treaty between the Commonwealth and the Cossack Host. Witness: Polish Wikisource transcription, "Ugoda hadziacka." Polish Wikisource.

The religious clause is striking: it treats the Greek Orthodox faith as a public, corporate right, not merely a private tolerance. That was a radical concession in a Commonwealth where Catholic renewal was already advancing. The fact that the treaty was not implemented is itself a historical fact: it shows the limits of what the Commonwealth’s political system could deliver, even when its negotiators were willing.

What if Hadziach had worked?

Counterfactuals are not evidence, but they sharpen the question. Hadziach would have created a Rus’ palatinate within the Commonwealth, possibly altering the later history of Ukraine and Poland-Lithuania. Its failure reminds us that treaties are not outcomes; they are promises whose fulfilment depends on power, will, and circumstance.

05 · Recovery

Recovery under the Vasas and Sobieski

The second half of the seventeenth century looked, at first, like recovery. Under Michael Korybut Wiśniowiecki, John III Sobieski, and the long reigns of the Wettin kings, the Commonwealth held off the Ottomans, recovered some prestige, and preserved its borders. Sobieski’s 1683 relief of Vienna made him a European hero and confirmed the Commonwealth’s reputation as the shield of Christendom.

Yet the recovery was shallow. The wars had depopulated vast regions, destroyed towns, and burdened the peasantry with heavier labour obligations. The Cossack frontier was divided; the Commonwealth’s eastern influence was permanently reduced. The great noble families had used the crisis to build private armies and client networks. The state that emerged from the Deluge was more aristocratic and less governable than the one that had entered it.

Cost-benefit analysis The victory at Vienna gave the Commonwealth European prestige. What did it cost in money, manpower, and opportunity? Was it a victory or a further drain on an already damaged state?

06 · Foreign rule

The Saxon kings and foreign interference

The election of Frederick Augustus of Saxony as Augustus II in 1697, and his son Augustus III in 1733, began a period of absentee monarchy and foreign patronage. The Saxon kings spent much of their time in Dresden; their Polish policy was shaped by Saxon, Russian, and Austrian interests. The Commonwealth became a field on which other powers played, buying votes, raising confederations, and vetoing reforms.

The most famous symbol of this weakness was the Silent Sejm of 1717, at which Russian troops surrounded the chamber and the envoys were forbidden to debate. The Sejm rubber-stamped decisions already agreed with Russia. Another symbol was the liberum veto: the practice that allowed a single deputy to dissolve the Sejm by declaring his veto. Originally intended to protect minority interests, by the eighteenth century it had become a tool of foreign money and magnate obstruction.

The Commonwealth did not lack reformers. Economists, lawyers, and soldiers wrote plans for stronger taxation, a standing army, and executive reform. But every proposal encountered a nobility that feared royal power more than it feared foreign domination. The result was a system that preserved liberty in form while losing sovereignty in practice.

Liberty or licence?

The liberum veto could be defended as a check on tyranny and attacked as a licence for anarchy. The same tension runs through the entire period: noble liberty was real and valuable, but its unbalanced exercise made the state vulnerable to anyone willing to pay for a veto.

07 · Society

The social order

The Commonwealth’s social pyramid was sharp. At the top stood the great magnates, whose estates were larger than some European states. Below them the broad szlachta—nobles with full legal rights but wildly varying wealth. Townspeople, legally inferior to nobles in most provinces, ran trade and crafts under magistrate charters. Peasants, the vast majority, owed labour services and dues to their lords and lived under manor courts. Jewish communities, organised through self-governing councils and kahals, occupied economic niches often closed to Christians.

The ideology of the noble republic celebrated equality among nobles and liberty under law. In reality, magnates dominated sejmiks, appointed officials, and controlled local armies of clients. The peasantry was increasingly burdened by corvée labour as noble estates sought to replace lost population and profit from grain exports. Women of all estates had property rights and could exercise influence through dowries, widowhood, and patronage, but they held no political office.

The Commonwealth’s celebrated freedom was thus an estate freedom. It was not universal, and it coexisted with forms of dependence that modern readers find hard to reconcile. The documents of the period rarely question this hierarchy; they assume it. To read them critically is to notice what is missing as much as what is said.

08 · Reform

The Enlightenment challenge

By the 1760s the Commonwealth was a protectorate in all but name. Catherine the Great had placed her candidate, Stanisław August Poniatowski, on the throne in 1764; Russian troops enforced his position. The same decade saw the Bar Confederation (1768–1772), a desperate noble rebellion against Russian domination, and the First Partition of 1772, in which Russia, Prussia, and Austria seized about a third of the Commonwealth’s territory.

Yet the catastrophe also produced reform. The young king surrounded himself with writers, economists, and painters; schools were modernised; the Commission of National Education, created in 1773, became the first European ministry of state education. Reformers argued that the Commonwealth could survive only by becoming a more efficient, centralised state—while preserving noble liberty. Their enemies, and many ordinary nobles, saw this as a betrayal of the old constitution.

The guarantee was a poisoned gift. Catherine’s promise to defend the Commonwealth’s existing constitution effectively meant that she would defend the very weakness that made Russia’s influence possible. Reformers were caught: to change the system was to invite Russian “protection” of the old order.

09 · Culture

Education, economy, and the public sphere

The Enlightenment did not reach the whole Commonwealth, but it reshaped its elite culture. The Commission of National Education reorganised the curricula of the Jagiellonian and Vilnius academies; the Collegium Nobilium in Warsaw trained a new generation of nobles in modern languages, science, and civic virtue. Journals, pamphlets, and coffee-house debates created a small but active public sphere.

The economy remained agrarian. Magnate estates exported grain through Gdańsk to western Europe; peasant labour obligations rose. Towns grew slowly, hampered by noble competition and legal privileges. Yet the period also produced important manufactories, mines, and a flourishing visual culture. The arts served the reform agenda: history paintings by Franciszek Smuglewicz and others presented the Commonwealth as a virtuous, ancient republic worth saving.

10 · Constitution

The Constitution of 3 May 1791

The Constitution of 3 May 1791 was the reformers’ masterpiece. It was adopted by a carefully prepared Sejm after two years of debate, and it transformed the Commonwealth into a hereditary constitutional monarchy with a strengthened executive council, a clearer separation of powers, and broader rights for townspeople. It was also the first modern written constitution in Europe and the second in the world, after the United States.

The reformers did not hide the stakes. The preamble speaks of the nation’s fate hanging on the perfection of its constitution, of the need to escape the “shameful dictates of foreign violence,” and of the determination to secure “external independence and internal liberty.” The king became a hereditary monarch from the Saxon Wettin line, but the real innovation was the Council of Guardians of the Laws—a cabinet-like executive responsible to the Sejm. The nobility’s equality was preserved; serfdom was acknowledged but limited; towns gained political representation and rights.

Primary source 6 · Constitution of 3 May 1791

Uznając, iż los nas wszystkich od ugruntowania i wydoskonalenia konstytucyi narodowej jedynie zawisł, długiem doświadczeniem poznawszy zadawnione rządu naszego wady, a chcąc korzystać z pory, w jakiej się Europa znajduje, i z tej dogorywającej chwili, która nas samym sobie wróciła; wolni od hańbiących obcej przemocy nakazów, ceniąc drożej nad życie, nad szczęśliwość osobistą, egzystencyę polityczną, niepodległość zewnętrzną i wolność wewnętrzną narodu, którego los w ręce nasze jest powierzonym; chcąc oraz na błogosławieństwo, na wdzięczność współczesnych i przyszłych pokoleń zasłużyć; mimo przeszkód, które w nas namiętności sprawować mogą, dla dobra powszechnego, dla ugruntowania wolności, dla ocalenia ojczyzny naszej i jej granic, z największą stałością ducha niniejszą konstytucyę uchwalamy; i tę całkowicie za świętą, za niewzruszoną deklarujemy.

Recognising that the fate of all of us depends solely on the establishment and perfection of the national constitution, having learned by long experience the ancient faults of our government, and wishing to profit from the moment in which Europe finds itself and from this failing moment which has returned us to ourselves; free from the shameful dictates of foreign violence, valuing more than life, more than personal happiness, political existence, external independence and internal liberty of the nation whose fate is entrusted to our hands; wishing also to earn the blessing and gratitude of present and future generations; despite the obstacles that passions may place in us, for the common good, for the establishment of liberty, for the salvation of our fatherland and its borders, with the greatest steadfastness of spirit we enact this constitution; and we declare it wholly sacred and inviolable.

Event: Warsaw, 3 May 1791. Composition: constitutional law adopted by the Great Sejm. Witness: Echo z nad Sanu (Przemyśl, 1885), public-domain reprint. Polish Wikisource.

The Constitution’s weakness was not in its design but in its environment. It was passed by a Sejm surrounded by reformers and the urban militia of Warsaw; its opponents were already turning to Russia. Within a year the Targowica Confederation would ask Catherine the Great to restore the old order. The Constitution was a reform in a state that no longer had the power to defend it.

Revolutionary or conservative?

The Constitution was both. It asserted national sovereignty and limited monarchy, but it preserved noble privilege and did not abolish serfdom. It was radical by the standards of the Commonwealth, moderate by the standards of revolutionary France. Its authors were not democrats; they were reforming aristocrats who believed that only a stronger state could preserve liberty.

11 · Reaction

The Targowica reaction

The Targowica Confederation, proclaimed in May 1792 at the Russian ambassador’s residence in Warsaw, was the Constitution’s most powerful domestic enemy. Its leaders came from the highest magnate families and the conservative clergy. They denounced the Constitution as a coup against the true liberties of the Commonwealth and appealed to Russia to restore the old order.

Historians have long debated whether the Targowica confederates were traitors or sincere conservatives. The act itself is framed in the language of constitutional restoration: it accuses the 1791 Sejm of usurping legislative power, trampling noble freedoms, and dragging the country into war with Russia. To its authors, the Constitution was the death of the republic; to its defenders, Targowica was the death of Polish sovereignty.

Primary source 7 · Act founding the Targowica Confederation, 14 May 1792

My senatorowie, ministrowie Rzczpltej, urzędnicy kor., tudzież urzędnicy, dygnitarze i rycerstwo kor., widząc, że już dla nas nie masz Rzczpltej, iż sejm dzisiejszy, na niedziel tylko sześć zwołany, przywłaszczywszy sobie władzę prawodawczą na zawsze, a już przeszło przez lat półczwarta ciągle ją ze wzgardą praw uzurpując, połamał prawa kardynale, zmiótł wszystkie wolności szlacheckiego, a na dniu 3 maja r. 1791 w rewolucję i spisek przemieniwszy się, nową formę rządu, za pomocą mieszczan, ułanów, żołnierzy, narzuconą sukcesję tronu postanowił, królowi od przysięgi, na pacta conventa wykonanej, uwolnić się dozwolił, władzę królów rozszerzył, rzeczpospolitą w monarchię zamienił, szlachtę bez posesji od równości i wolności odepchnął… w wojnę szkodliwą przeciwko Rosji, sąsiadki naszej najlepszej, najdawniejszej z przyjaciół i sprzymierzeńców naszych, wplątać nas usiłował.

We senators, ministers of the Commonwealth, Crown officials, and also officials, dignitaries and knighthood of the Crown, seeing that for us the Commonwealth no longer exists, since the present Sejm, summoned for only six weeks, having arrogated to itself legislative power for ever, and for more than four years continuously usurping it in contempt of the laws, has broken the cardinal laws, swept away all noble liberties, and on the day of 3 May 1791, turning into revolution and conspiracy, established a new form of government, with the help of burghers, uhlans, and soldiers, imposed hereditary succession, allowed the king to free himself from the oath sworn in the pacta conventa, extended royal power, transformed the republic into a monarchy, pushed nobles without property from equality and liberty… and strove to entangle us in a harmful war against Russia, our best, oldest of friends and allies.

Event: Targowica, 14 May 1792. Composition: confederation act. Witness: Polish Wikisource transcription, “Akt założenia konfederacji targowickiej.” Polish Wikisource.

With Russian armies advancing and conservative nobles rallying to Targowica, the reformers’ coalition collapsed. The king joined the confederation rather than fight a hopeless war. The Constitution was effectively annulled. In 1793 the Second Partition took away another slice of territory, and the Commonwealth was reduced to a Russian client state.

12 · Uprising

Kościuszko’s Uprising, 1794

The Kościuszko Uprising was the last attempt to save the Commonwealth by arms. Tadeusz Kościuszko, a veteran of the American War of Independence and a military engineer of high reputation, was appointed Supreme Commander of the National Armed Forces. He issued the Manifesto of Polaniec on 7 May 1794, which promised to limit peasant labour obligations and protect the rural poor in exchange for their support against Russia and Prussia.

The uprising had brilliant moments: the Polish victory at Racławice in April 1794, the capture of the Warsaw arsenal by the urban population, and the Lithuanian insurrection led by Jakub Jasiński. But it also faced the full military power of Russia, Prussia, and Austria. After the defeat of Maciejowice in October, where Kościuszko was wounded and captured, the uprising crumbled. The Russian assault on Warsaw in November ended organized resistance.

Primary source 8 · Kościuszko’s Polaniec Manifesto, 7 May 1794

Od tych którzy będą wezwani na Pospolite Ruszenie, poki tylko będą zostawać pod bronią, Pańszczyzna przez ten czas nie będzie wyciągana, lecz dopiero rozpocznie się od powrotu ich do domu. Własność posiadanego gruntu z obowiązkami do niego przywiązanemi podług wyżey wyrażoney ulgi, nie może bydź od Dziedzica żadnemu Włościanowi odięta, chybaby się wprzod o to przed Dozorcą mieyscowym rozprawił i dowiodł że Włościanin obowiązkom swoim, zadość nie czyni. Wzaiemnie Lud Wieyski doznaiąc sprawiedliwości i dobroci Rządu, powinien gorliwie pozostałe dni Pańszczyzny odbywać, Zwierzchności swoiey bydź pośłuśnym, gospodarstwa pilnować, role dobrze uprawiać i zasiewać, a gdy takowa ulga uczyniona jest dla Włościan z pobudek ratunku Oyczyzny, i Właściciele przez miłość Oyczyzny chętnie ią przyimuią, przeto Włościanie nie maią się wymawiać od naymów potrzebnych Dworom za przyzwoitą zapłatą.

From those who are summoned to the general levy, for as long as they remain under arms, labour service will not be exacted, but will begin only upon their return home. The property of the land held, with the obligations attached to it according to the above-mentioned relief, cannot be taken from any peasant by the landlord, unless he first pleads and proves before the local overseer that the peasant is not fulfilling his obligations. In turn, the rural people, experiencing the justice and goodness of the Government, should diligently perform the remaining days of labour service, be obedient to their authority, attend to their households, cultivate and sow their fields well; and since this relief is granted to the peasants for the sake of the salvation of the Fatherland, and the landowners willingly accept it out of love for the Fatherland, the peasants should not refuse the necessary labour for the manors at a fair wage.

Event: Polaniec, 7 May 1794. Composition: manifesto of the Supreme Commander of the National Armed Forces. Witness: Polish Wikisource transcription, “Uniwersał Urządzający Włościan…”. Polish Wikisource.

The manifesto was limited. It did not abolish serfdom or give peasants the land; it offered temporary relief and legal protection in exchange for military service. Yet for the conservative nobility it was dangerously radical. It shows how far the reformers had moved since 1791: they now understood that the Commonwealth could not be defended without widening the circle of those who had a stake in its survival.

Moral dilemma Kościuszko’s manifesto asked peasants to fight for a state that had long oppressed them. Was the offer fair? Was it enough? Who bore the greater risk—the noble insurgents or the peasant soldiers?

13 · Collapse

The end of the Commonwealth

The defeat of the Kościuszko Uprising removed the last obstacle to the Third Partition. In 1795 Russia, Prussia, and Austria divided the remaining lands. The king abdicated in November; the Commonwealth ceased to exist. Warsaw became a Prussian city; Kraków fell to Austria; Vilnius to Russia.

The partitions were not a natural disaster. They were a diplomatic and military outcome of a state that had lost the capacity to defend its borders and the unity to reform itself. The Commonwealth’s neighbours did not invent its weaknesses, but they exploited them with patience and precision. By the end of the century, a political culture that had prized liberty above coercion had been defeated by states that had learned to coerce more effectively.

Was the Commonwealth doomed?

Historians answer differently. Some stress structural failure: a weak state, a veto-based legislature, and an economy that could not match its rivals. Others stress contingency: the partitions required a particular combination of Russian ambition, Prussian growth, and Austrian opportunism. The sources in this companion support both views; they also show that the Commonwealth’s own leaders were aware of the danger and, at the very end, tried to escape it.

14 · Towns

Towns and trade

Towns in the Commonwealth were legally and economically complex. Royal cities, private towns, and church towns operated under different charters. Gdańsk, the great Hanseatic port, was semi-autonomous and closely tied to Dutch and English trade. Warsaw grew from a provincial residence into a capital under the Saxon and then Poniatowski kings. Lwów, Wilno, and Kraków remained centres of law, learning, and commerce.

Yet the urban economy was constrained. The nobility could manufacture and sell goods on their estates without paying the taxes imposed on towns. Serf labour on estates depressed wages for free labourers. Guilds restricted entry. Reformers repeatedly tried to give townspeople the same rights as nobles, but the Constitution’s urban provisions were among the first to be reversed. The towns supported reform because reform offered them equality; the nobility feared that equality as the end of their privileged world.

15 · Jewish life

The Jewish Commonwealth within

The Commonwealth was home to the largest Jewish population in Europe. Jewish communities enjoyed extensive self-government through councils, kahals, and rabbinic courts. They spoke Yiddish, prayed in Hebrew, and lived under royal charters that guaranteed residence, trade, and communal autonomy. They were also periodically blamed for economic grievances and subjected to violence, especially during the upheavals of 1648–1649 and the Deluge.

The Jewish experience was not uniform. Wealthy merchants and leaseholders were tied to noble magnates; poor artisans and tavern-keepers lived in villages and small towns. The communal structure gave Jewish subjects a collective voice that many Christian townspeople lacked, but it also made them visible targets. The modern historiography of the period, drawing on Jewish communal records and Polish court archives, shows a population that was both integrated and vulnerable.

16 · Memory

Culture, memory, and religion

The Commonwealth’s last century was also a creative one. Baroque and then Enlightenment culture produced literature, music, architecture, and painting. The Catholic Church remained powerful, but the Greek Catholic, Orthodox, Lutheran, Calvinist, and Jewish communities maintained distinct cultures. The struggle over church union and the rights of the Orthodox continued to shape politics in the east.

Memory was contested from the start. The defeat of the Deluge was remembered as a miracle of national survival; the partitions as a martyrdom. Romantic historians and poets of the nineteenth century turned the Commonwealth into a symbol of lost liberty and national suffering. That memory is powerful, but it is not the same as the historical record. This companion treats the period as a field of choice and consequence, not as a prelude to later national narratives.

17 · Method

Source criticism

Every source in this companion has a point of view. The 1648 General Confederation speaks the language of a state trying to restore order; Khmelnytsky’s letter speaks the language of a wronged loyalist. The Lwów Oath is a royal vow, not a social contract. The Hadziach Treaty records promises that were never kept. The 1768 Russian treaty uses the language of friendship to describe a guarantee that was in fact a limitation. The Constitution of 3 May is a reform manifesto; the Targowica act is its denunciation; the Polaniec Manifesto is a military appeal to the peasantry.

These documents are not neutral evidence. They are interventions in political struggles. They must be read alongside their contexts: military events, social structures, and the rivalries of estates and empires. The translations aim to be faithful to the originals, including their legal formulas and ideological assumptions, without endorsing those assumptions. Where a source is incomplete or from a later edition, this is noted.

The images are also sources in a sense. A nineteenth-century painting by Matejko or Kossak is not a window into the 1650s or 1790s; it is a reconstruction made for a later public. The captions therefore distinguish contemporary portraits, modern maps, and retrospective paintings. Readers should not confuse art for evidence.

18 · Glossary

Terms and concepts

Commonwealth
Translation of Rzeczpospolita; the Polish-Lithuanian state, officially a republic with an elected king.
Sejm
The national parliament, composed of the king, the senate, and the chamber of envoys.
Sejmik
A local diet of the nobility, which elected deputies and instructed them.
Liberum veto
The practice that allowed one deputy to dissolve the Sejm by declaring his veto; formally abolished in 1791.
Szlachta
The nobility; legally equal to one another, though economically diverse.
Magnate
A great landowner, often with private armies and client networks.
Cossack
A member of the Zaporozhian Host, a frontier military community on the Dnieper.
Hetman
The highest military commander of the Commonwealth or the Cossack Host.
Corvée
Unpaid labour service owed by peasants to their lords; called pańszczyzna in Polish.
Partition
The division of the Commonwealth’s territory among Russia, Prussia, and Austria in 1772, 1793, and 1795.
Confederation
An armed association of nobles, often formed for political or military purposes.
Guard of Laws
The executive council created by the Constitution of 1791, precursor to a modern cabinet.

20 · Study

Study questions

  1. Compare the General Confederation of 1648 with the Constitution of 1791. How did the Commonwealth’s understanding of its own problems change?
  2. Was Khmelnytsky a rebel, a loyalist, or something else? What evidence can you draw from his letter?
  3. Why did the Hadziach Treaty fail? What does its failure suggest about the limits of compromise in the Commonwealth?
  4. How did the 1768 Russian “guarantee” restrict rather than protect Polish liberty?
  5. What were the strongest and weakest points of the Constitution of 3 May?
  6. Did the Targowica confederates have a valid constitutional argument, or were they simply Russian clients?
  7. What did Kościuszko offer the peasants, and what did he ask in return?
  8. Was the Commonwealth’s collapse caused by internal weakness, foreign aggression, or both? Use sources from the companion to support your answer.
  9. How does a nineteenth-century painting by Matejko or Kossak shape our memory of the seventeenth century?
  10. What does this period suggest about the relationship between liberty and state power?
